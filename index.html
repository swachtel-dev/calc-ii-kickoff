§ I The product

Calc II is part of Pillars, a new Rize product line for traditional gen-ed courses delivered online at scale across partner colleges. Pillars differs from our existing career-track courses: it is exam-primary, textbook-driven, and structurally uniform week to week.

Because it's the first gen-ed build, we're optimizing for ship to spec fast, then iterate from student data rather than perfecting in theory. The constraints are real, but so is the freedom to make judgment calls.

§ IV How each unit works

Every week follows the same structure. Students are never surprised by what a unit will ask of them. A unit is at most four things.

  1. Textbook reading. A link to the relevant section of Paul's Online Math Notes, our textbook. No redundant re-explanation; Paul's treatment is excellent.
  2. Problem Set. Graded, submitted work. Machine grading is explicitly provisional: a correct final answer with no supporting work earns zero. Students must show their reasoning.
  3. Supplementary resources. some weeks. Optional video walkthroughs, alternative explanations, worked-example banks. This is where your preferred explanations live.
  4. Exam. W7 midterm, W14 final. Three-section format: auto-gradable items, short answer, long answer. The midterm mirrors the final's structure exactly, so students rehearse the format before the real thing.
Students also have access to a practice tool I built, which we're calling the batting cage. It is a drill-style environment with hundreds of additional problems, worked solutions, and a timer. Ungraded. Students use it to build reps outside of graded work. You can try it here.

§ V Five principles

  1. Clarity over cleverness. Students know what each week asks of them. No surprises, no hidden rules.
  2. Consistent structure. Every unit has the same shape. The midterm looks like the final.
  3. Practice is the pedagogy. Worked examples and problem sets, not passive reading. Reps matter.
  4. Work is respected. Show-your-work is non-negotiable. Machine grading is always provisional.
  5. Ship, then iterate. Build to spec fast. Optimize from real student data, not theory.
